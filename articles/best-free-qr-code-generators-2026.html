What Is a QR Code and Why Do You Need One?

A QR (Quick Response) code is a two-dimensional barcode that can store various types of data. When scanned with a smartphone camera, QR codes can instantly direct users to websites, display text, initiate phone calls, send emails, or connect to WiFi networks.

Here's why QR codes have become indispensable in 2026:

  • Contactless interactions ‚Äî Share information without physical contact
  • Instant access ‚Äî Users can access content in seconds
  • Versatility ‚Äî Works for URLs, vCards, WiFi, payments, and more
  • Trackable ‚Äî Dynamic QR codes can track scan analytics
  • Mobile-first ‚Äî Every smartphone has a built-in QR scanner

Types of QR Codes You Can Create

Modern QR code generators support various data types. Here's what you can encode:

URL QR Codes

The most common type ‚Äî link directly to any website. Perfect for marketing materials, business cards, and product packaging. When scanned, the user's browser opens the specified URL.

WiFi QR Codes

Share WiFi credentials without typing passwords. The QR code contains the network name (SSID), password, and encryption type. Guests can connect by simply scanning the code.

vCard QR Codes

Digital business cards that add contact information directly to the phone's address book. Include name, phone, email, company, and more.

Plain Text QR Codes

Display any text message when scanned. Useful for instructions, serial numbers, or short messages.

Email QR Codes

Pre-fill an email with recipient, subject line, and body text. Great for customer feedback or support requests.

SMS QR Codes

Open the messaging app with a pre-filled phone number and message. Useful for text-to-vote campaigns or quick contact.

QR Code Best Practices

Creating a QR code is easy, but creating an effective QR code requires some thought. Follow these best practices:

Size Matters

Ensure your QR code is large enough to scan. As a rule of thumb, the scanning distance should be about 10 times the width of the QR code. A 1-inch code works well from about 10 inches away.

Contrast is Key

QR codes need high contrast to scan reliably. Dark codes on light backgrounds work best. Avoid low-contrast color combinations like light gray on white or yellow on orange.

Error Correction

QR codes include built-in error correction. Higher error correction (H level) allows the code to remain scannable even if up to 30% is damaged, but results in denser codes. Lower levels (L) create simpler codes but are less resilient.

Test Before Printing

Always test your QR code with multiple devices before printing. What works on your phone might not work on older devices.

Provide Context

Add a call-to-action near your QR code. "Scan to connect to WiFi" or "Scan for menu" tells users what to expect.

Static vs. Dynamic QR Codes

Understanding the difference between static and dynamic QR codes is crucial:

Static QR Codes

Static codes encode data directly. Once created, the content cannot change. If you encode a URL and later want to change it, you need a new QR code. However, static codes are:

  • Completely free (no ongoing costs)
  • Work forever without any service dependency
  • Cannot be disabled or taken down
  • No scan tracking capabilities

Dynamic QR Codes

Dynamic codes redirect through a service that can track scans and allow URL changes. Benefits include:

  • Change destination without reprinting
  • Track scan counts, locations, and devices
  • A/B testing capabilities

However, dynamic codes typically require paid subscriptions and depend on the service remaining operational.
